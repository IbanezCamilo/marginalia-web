# Product

## Mission

Marginalia is a home for literary writing in Spanish. Where the large platforms treat writing as content to be ranked, optimized, and consumed, Marginalia treats it as work to be read with attention and kept.

The ambition is unfashionably large: to become, over time, a place that helps carry Spanish-language literary culture — the role a good magazine or a small press once played — warm, curated, and personal where the giants are cold by design. That coldness is not an accident of theirs; it is what their economics require. This is precisely the ground they cannot stand on, and therefore ours to hold.

The ambition is deliberately staged. Marginalia begins as one person's project and is built to grow with more hands — editors, curators, eventually commissioned writers — without losing the warmth that makes it worth building. **"Personal" describes the experience, not the org chart.** The reader should always feel that a human chose this, arranged this, cared about this. That feeling has to survive scale, because it is the whole point — not a phase to outgrow.

> If this framing is wrong and Marginalia is meant to stay a solo, unscaled personal garden, this section changes entirely — and so does everything about distribution and sustainability below. The rest of the document assumes "personal in feel, built to grow."

## Users

**Literary readers** — people with genuine interest in literature who read carefully, save texts, and return. They arrive with intention, not by accident. They notice typography, appreciate unhurried layout, and expect the reading experience to honor the writing.

**Writers/authors** — writers who publish their work through the admin panel. They spend real time inside the editor: drafting, revising, categorizing, managing their archive. The authoring workspace must feel as considered as the reading side, not like a utility bolted on.

Both groups share a context: they are comfortable with language, attentive to craft, and would notice if the product felt careless.

## Brand Personality

Warm, curious, accessible.

Warm but never cozy-kitsch. Curious in the sense of a reader who underlines passages. Accessible meaning literature should feel inviting, not exclusive — this is not a site for gatekeepers.

Voice is Spanish, conversational but precise. Sentences are earned. No filler. Reads like a well-edited essay.

A note on "accessible, not gatekeepers": Marginalia is curated, and curation is a form of gate. We resolve the tension deliberately — **open in spirit, curated in practice.** Anyone can read; anyone can ask to write. What is gated is not access or belonging but the editorial bar for publication. The gate exists to protect the reader's trust, never to signal exclusivity.

## References

We are defined as much by what we admire as by what we refuse.

**What we look toward** — the literary review and the well-made book: an editorial point of view, a sense that someone is home, typography that respects the reader, selection as an act of care. *(Name here the two or three specific publications, presses, or objects Marginalia should sit beside — positive references orient better than a list of enemies, and only you can choose them.)*

**What we look away from — as trade-offs, not taboos.** Each refusal below has a cost. We name the cost so the decision stays honest and can be revisited if the cost ever becomes fatal.

- **Growth-hacking aesthetics** — subscription popups, "join 10,000 readers" banners, aggressive email capture, engagement nags. *Cost of refusing:* slower list growth, no built-in viral loop. *Accepted because* the manipulation is visible and it would corrode the calm the product exists to protect. **Important distinction:** we refuse the *hustle*, not the *bridge*. Reaching a reader again — by RSS, a sober opt-in, a quiet "follow this writer" — is not the sin. Hustling them is. (See Distribution & Return.)
- **Generic blog / SEO aesthetics** — sidebars, widget stacks, public post-count badges that exist to look busy. *Cost of refusing:* less optimized for search-engine discovery, less "active"-looking. *Accepted because* the information hierarchy is editorial, not SEO-driven.
- **Advertising.** *Cost of refusing:* forgoing the easiest revenue on the internet. *Accepted because* it contradicts "the platform disappears" — ads would fund the product by destroying the exact thing that differentiates it. This one is not a trade-off we intend to revisit.

## Design Principles

**The text rules.** Typography carries the experience. Every layout decision defers to readability and rhythm. Decorative elements exist only when they serve the writing, never the other way around.

**Reading and writing share DNA.** The public reading experience and the authoring workspace share the same visual language. Writers should feel the same warmth in the editor that readers feel on the blog. No jarring context switch.

**Light and shadow in balance.** Neither light nor dark mode is the default aesthetic. Both are designed with equal intention — light for the day reader who wants clarity and warmth, dark for the night reader who wants immersion. Neither is a fallback of the other.

**Accessible without sacrificing quality.** WCAG AA is the baseline, not a constraint. High contrast, readable type, and keyboard navigability make the experience better for everyone, not just users with specific needs.

**The platform disappears.** Marginalia should feel invisible when it's working.

## Distribution & Return

The mission is impossible if no one finds Marginalia and no one ever comes back. Discovery and return are therefore first-class concerns — not growth tactics to be avoided, but obligations the mission imposes. You cannot be the home of a literature that no one can find and no one can revisit.

The governing rule is not "no distribution." It is: **distribution a careful reader would welcome.**

- **Reading is always free and open.** A reader can send a friend a link and the friend can read it. A home for a literature cannot live behind a wall.
- **A quiet way back.** A reader can follow a writer or a subject and be told, without noise, when there is something new — by RSS or a sober email digest, never by a badge, a streak, or a nag.
- **Discovery is editorial before it is algorithmic.** A curated front page, hand-made selections, and real paths between related texts — not an engagement-optimized feed. Curation is the personality; the algorithm, if any, only assists it.

**Metrics follow the same logic.** No public vanity counts. A writer may privately see that they were read — because silence discourages good writers as surely as vanity metrics corrupt them — but the reader's experience is never shaped by what is trending.

## Sustainability

Marginalia intends to sustain itself. Honesty requires admitting that the things which carry a literature — reviews, small presses, journals — rarely pay for themselves while they are small; they are almost always subsidized on the way up. The model is therefore a **sequence, not a single choice**, and reading stays free at every stage.

1. **Now — free, open, subsidized.** Reading is free. Early costs are carried by the founder and, where possible, by cultural grants or patronage that let us commission the first writers and seed the catalog. The goal of this stage is not revenue; it is reaching enough good work that the rest becomes possible.
2. **Then — membership and patronage.** As a small, loyal readership forms, voluntary support becomes the recurring base — framed as sustaining a cultural project, in the spirit of a patron, not as unlocking a paywall.
3. **Later — author-side tools.** At scale, mature monetization comes from the writing side: professional tools, portfolio and custom-domain features, private analytics, export. The reader is never the one asked to pay for access.

**Two lines we will not cross:** reading behind a paywall (it contradicts the mission) and advertising (it contradicts the experience). Everything else is negotiable as the project learns what it can afford.
